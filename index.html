What Does It Mean to Write a History of India?

Oke School · First Note

Every generation rewrites India’s past—not because the past changes, but because the questions we ask of it do.

Writing a history of India is not the same as listing events in chronological order. It involves decisions about geography, language, sources, and scale. The subcontinent predates the modern nation-state by centuries, and any attempt to narrate its past must confront this tension.

Historians rely on surviving materials: inscriptions, court records, travel accounts, administrative documents, and literary texts. What survives often reflects power—who had the resources to record their world, and whose lives were considered worth preserving.

History as Method

Audrey Truschke’s book India offers one way of approaching this problem. Rather than presenting a civilizational essence, it focuses on how evidence is read and interpreted. Persian chronicles, for example, are treated not as neutral accounts but as texts shaped by political contexts.

This approach unsettles readers who expect history to confirm inherited narratives. Academic distance can feel like detachment, and textual analysis can seem to erase lived memory. Yet this discomfort reveals a deeper conflict about what history is supposed to do.

Perhaps the question is not whether a single history of India is correct, but why we continue to search for one. The persistence of disagreement suggests that history functions less as a settled account and more as an ongoing argument about meaning, authority, and memory.
